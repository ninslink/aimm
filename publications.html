ABSTRACT

Timely assessments of child acute malnutrition are essential for effective treatment and prevention of undernutrition. Yet current screening systems are often costly, infrequent, and dependent on trained personnel and physical equipment. To address these limitations, we developed D2A, a smartphone-based application that enables caregivers to repeatedly self-collect Family MUAC measurements and household-level risk information linked to child wasting. The system combines simplified mobile interfaces with app-guided workflows designed for low-resource settings. Based on a seven-month pilot study involving 180 households in West Pokot County, Kenya, we evaluate the acceptance, accuracy, and operational cost of app-based self-screening both with and without the support of Community Health Volunteers. We compare the performance of D2A against conventional paper-based community nutrition screening approaches and assess the feasibility of household-operated digital monitoring systems for longitudinal child nutrition surveillance.

ABSTRACT

Insight into the resilience of local food systems is critical for understanding how children and households withstand repeated exposure to drought, food insecurity, conflict, and economic shocks. Existing approaches often rely on static indicators that fail to capture how vulnerability evolves over time. In this study, we use latent class mixed modeling and time-to-event analysis to develop and validate a longitudinal measure of resilience to acute malnutrition in the Kenyan drylands. Drawing on repeated observations from community-level nutrition surveillance systems, we examine how trajectories of wasting risk differ across households and regions and assess how resilience changes in response to recurrent stressors. Our approach captures both the frequency and severity of acute malnutrition episodes and provides a framework for identifying populations that remain persistently vulnerable despite periods of apparent recovery.

Trajectories of resilience to acute malnutrition in the Kenyan drylands

DOIPublisher
ABSTRACT

Anticipating which households are most at risk of child acute malnutrition is essential for timely intervention and effective resource allocation during food crises. However, nutritional vulnerability is shaped not only by environmental conditions but also by complex behavioral and social dynamics within households. Using longitudinal household data collected across 23 counties in Kenya between 2016 and 2020, we develop, calibrate, and validate an evidence-driven computational model to investigate how variation in household behavior influences vulnerability to acute malnutrition. The model integrates information on livelihoods, coping strategies, food consumption, and social adaptation to simulate how households respond to repeated stressors over time. Our findings demonstrate that differences in household-level decision-making substantially influence nutritional outcomes and that computational social science approaches can improve the prediction of acute malnutrition risk under conditions of uncertainty.

Interview: AIMM and the Center for Artificial Intelligence

Hello Nina, thank you for agreeing to do the interview. You have been collaborating with our Centre to develop the AI-based malnutrition detection system for children.

Could you share a bit about the broader aims of this work and what aspects of this project do you find most interesting?

The Artificial Intelligence for Monitoring Malnutrition project aims to make the early detection of child undernutrition faster, more accurate, and more accessible, especially in low-resource or crisis settings where conventional screening tools are difficult to use at scale. What excites me most is how AIMM bridges AI and infrared imaging with deep local collaboration in Maharashtra. By empowering caregivers to monitor their children’s nutrition independently, AIMM broadens access to health data and supports timely, community-led interventions.

Traditional anthropometric methods such as weight-for-height z-scores and mid-upper arm circumference require trained personnel, calibrated equipment, and direct physical access to children. Data collection is expensive and infrequent. AIMM addresses these challenges through co-design, local language support, audio prompts, and training with community health workers.

In the summer of 2025, the team carried out a pilot study with FLAME University student interns and collected data from around 850 children in Satara district, Maharashtra. The new four-year project expands that work toward household-led monitoring over a 12-month period.

Interview: AIMM and the Center for Artificial Intelligence (CAI)

Hello Nina, thank you for agreeing to do the interview. You have been collaborating with our Centre to develop the AI-based malnutrition detection system for children.

Could you share a bit about the broader aims of this work and what aspects of this project do you find most interesting?

The Artificial Intelligence for Monitoring Malnutrition (AIMM) project aims to make the early detection of child undernutrition faster, more accurate, and more accessible—especially in low-resource or crisis settings where conventional screening tools are difficult to use at scale. What excites me most is how AIMM bridges cutting-edge AI and infrared imaging technology with deep local collaboration in India’s Maharashtra state. It’s not just a technological innovation—it’s a social one. By empowering caregivers, particularly mothers, to monitor their children’s nutrition independently, AIMM helps democratize access to health data and promotes timely, community-led interventions. We also see broader potential: AIMM’s approach could be deployed in humanitarian emergencies—say, in Gaza, where non-invasive, smartphone-based tools could provide rapid assessments of children’s health when access and infrastructure are severely constrained. Together with Prof. Ravi Bhavnani from the Geneva Graduate Institute, we have recently been awarded a four-year BRIDGE Discovery grant from the Swiss National Science Foundation to advance this work in partnership with the Center for Interdisciplinary Artificial Intelligence (CAI) and the Center for Research in Well-being and Happiness at FLAME. This funding allows us to move from proof-of-concept to real-world deployment.

Why is detecting malnutrition in children through available methods so challenging? Are there any technical or logistical barriers that make this work difficult?

Yes—there are many. Traditional anthropometric methods such as Weight-for-Height Z-scores (WHZ) and Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC) require trained personnel, calibrated equipment, and direct physical access to children, which can be difficult in remote areas or during crises. Data collection is also expensive and infrequent, often relying on national surveys conducted every five years, meaning that emerging malnutrition hotspots go undetected for long periods. But beyond these technical challenges, there are social barriers to uptake that are just as critical. In many rural or crisis-affected regions, trust in digital technology is low, literacy levels are limited, and caregivers—especially women—may have little or no experience using smartphones. Also, in many places, people remain understandably cautious about image-based data collection, worrying about privacy or misuse. Others face very practical obstacles, such as poor internet access or shared phone use. AIMM addresses these challenges through co-design and training: we build the app together with caregivers, in local languages, using audio prompts and visual cues for those who can’t read. We also train community health workers to build confidence and trust around the technology. The goal isn’t just to build accurate AI—it’s to make sure people actually want to use it. In my PhD research in Kenya, I piloted an intuitive smartphone app with 180 households in West Pokot, showing that when digital tools are co-designed with communities, they can achieve accuracy comparable to conventional surveys at significantly lower cost, while improving community engagement and female empowerment.

Can you walk us through a typical data collection process for such a project? What methods do you usually use for the task?

In the summer of 2025, we carried out a pilot study at FLAME University with a wonderful team of 5 SIP student interns from different departments. Together, we collected data from around 850 children in Satara district, Maharashtra, to build a proof of concept for the AIMM system. Each child’s data included both hand-measured indicators—like height, weight, and mid-upper arm circumference—and corresponding images. We collected data in real field conditions to capture real-world variation in lighting, angles, and device quality. All images were anonymized, geo-coded, and securely stored. This gave us a rich dataset to train and test the first version of our AI model. We have already begun internal validation by comparing image-based estimates to hand-measured anthropometric data to assess model accuracy and bias. In the new four-year project, data collection will expand to household-led monitoring over a 12-month period, enabling families themselves to capture and upload images through the AIMM app—transforming the process from researcher-driven to truly community-based nutrition surveillance.

What considerations shape the way child health data, particularly images, are gathered?

Our guiding principles are ethics, usability, and accuracy. Ethically, we follow strict consent and anonymization protocols, compliant with GDPR and local regulations. In terms of usability, the app’s interface is co-designed with caregivers to ensure it’s intuitive and accessible, including visual cues and multilingual audio support for non-literate users. For accuracy, we standardize lighting and image distance where possible, and the AI model accounts for skin tone and age diversity. The infrared imaging approach also ensures privacy and inclusivity—children’s faces aren’t stored or transmitted, and the app can be used confidently by caregivers even in sensitive contexts.

What impact do you believe the AI-based detection system will have on your work? What outcomes from this collaboration would signal success to you?

A validated AI-based detection system could completely change how we monitor child nutrition. If AIMM performs as well as, or better than, traditional anthropometric tools—while being much cheaper and easier to use—it could allow communities to detect and address malnutrition before it becomes severe. The real power of this technology is that it turns monitoring into something proactive and local, not just a large-scale survey exercise done every few years. For me, success would mean two things: first, that the system works reliably across different sub-populations, in particular casts, and second, that it’s genuinely adopted by caregivers and health workers. Ideally, AIMM would become a trusted part of community health systems in places like Maharashtra and eventually be integrated into government initiatives such as Poshan Abhiyaan. Because the app is open-source, it can be adapted anywhere, from refugee camps to rural clinics, enabling real-time, community-led nutrition monitoring around the world.

How do you feel the recent AI boom will affect social science and anthropological research?

AI is transforming the way we do research. It allows us to identify large-scale patterns in health, behavior, and inequality that would be impossible to spot manually, but it also risks oversimplifying complex social realities. Data can tell us a lot, but it can’t explain why people act the way they do—or what those choices mean in their social and political environment. That’s why I think the future lies in blending AI’s analytical power with the depth of social science. That balance—between technology and human understanding—is exactly what we’re trying to achieve with AIMM.
